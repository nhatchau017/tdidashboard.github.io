Transdiagnostic Digital Intervention (TDI) is a project that seeks to address the co-occurring symptoms among adolescent and young adult (AYA; 15–24 years) childhood cancer survivors (CCS) using Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT).

This methodology governs the Phase 2 market scan of the TDI project. The scan identifies and characterises CBT-based digital interventions for symptoms that co-occur in survivors of childhood cancer, including but are not limited to anxiety, depression, pain, fatigue. Scan findings inform downstream Phase 3, 4, and 5 deliverables, including gap analysis, persona-aligned feature synthesis, low-fidelity design decisions, and co-design facilitation materials. Apps are not procured through this scan; the scan produces a design-reference corpus.

Scope is defined at the level of the intervention modality (CBT-based digital apps) rather than at the level of the intended user population. Apps designed for AYA CCS are captured as a named category within the scan, alongside apps designed for other populations whose design choices are transferable. Technologies in and out of scope are defined below to ensure consistent and defensible inclusion decisions. The TDI Theoretical Framework v1.0 is the authoritative source for the theoretical constructs this methodology references, not restated, in Section 2.0.

1.0 Scope and Eligibility Criteria

1.1 Inclusion Criteria

  • Mobile-delivered digital intervention. Includes standalone apps and SDKs deployable as apps. AND/OR
  • Web-delivered digital intervention. Includes standalone websites or hybrid web-and-app products.
  • Delivers CBT content. Presence of at least one core component of CBT protocol: psychoeducation, cognitive reappraisal, behavioural activation, relaxation strategies, problem-solving skills.
  • Transdiagnostic intervention covering ≥2 symptoms common to AYA CCS (anxiety, depression, pain, fatigue, etc.). Accessible at the time of scan via (a) public Apple App Store / Google Play, (b) institutional / clinical deployment with public documentation, or (c) active research trial with a named deployment pathway.

1.2 Exclusion Criteria

  • Delivers no CBT content (mindfulness/ACT/meditation-only).
  • Clinician-facing tools only with no user-facing component.
  • Withdrawn from market or abandoned (no updates within last 24 months AND no current download/deployment pathway).
  • Requires external hardware (wearables, biosensors) as the primary delivery or measurement mechanism.

1.3 Scan Categories

Within-scope apps are tagged across five category axes (extraction facets, not scope filters): mechanisms, symptom coverage, delivery model, design features, population target. Full operationalisation lives in the companion Extraction Schema document.

1.3.1 Mechanisms

  • CBT principles used: cognitive restructuring, behavioural activation, graded exposure, problem-solving, relaxation, psychoeducation, self-monitoring, relapse prevention, guided discovery.
  • Other mental wellness tooling: mindfulness, ACT, DBT, MI, positive psychology, peer support, expressive writing.

1.3.2 Symptom Coverage

Open-coded list including anxiety, depression, pain, fatigue, sleep disturbance, cancer-related cognitive impairment, social isolation, rumination, health anxiety. Original product vocabulary retained.

1.3.3 Delivery Model

  • Self-guided
  • Therapist-guided
  • Hybrid (blended)
  • AI-mediated

1.3.4 Design Features

  • Gamification elements
  • Just-in-time adaptive interventions for symptom flares
  • Personalised symptom-cluster identification
  • CBT modules tailored to cancer-specific concerns
  • CBT modules for chronic pain
  • Health anxiety coping tools
  • Guided healthcare transition content
  • Modules on disclosing cancer history; health/symptom/medication tracking calibrated to late effects
  • Moderated peer support; multimedia content; story-mapping (diary/journal); glossaries
  • Customisation to CCS or individual diagnoses

1.3.5 Population Target

Stated user population captured as expressed by the product (e.g., "AYA cancer survivors", "adolescents with chronic pain", "general adult mental health"). Age ranges should fall between 15 and 24 for primary AYA targeting.

5.0 AI-Assisted Data Collection

5.1 Approach and Rationale

An AI system (Anthropic Claude) performs app identification, data extraction across the five scan categories, and preliminary scoring across the eight dimensions. Recognised by Cochrane / Campbell / JBI / CEE joint position statement (Flemyng et al., 2025) when accompanied by human validation; the scan's governance model meets CDA-AMC (2024) AI-evaluation criteria.

5.2 Search Protocol

AI executes Mechanism × Symptom search combinations defined in the extraction workbook. Each app is profiled using the standardised Extraction Schema. When data are unavailable, fields are marked "Unknown" rather than imputed and flagged for human review per Section 5.3.

5.3 Validation and Quality Assurance

  • Source verification — every data point includes a citation (URL + access date); reviewers verify ≥3 random data points per app across different field domains.
  • Scoring review — all AI-generated scores reviewed by ≥1 human reviewer; adjustments documented.
  • Gap follow-up — fields marked "Unknown" flagged for vendor engagement (especially for apps tentatively classified as Strong Design Reference).
  • Manual scoring of NPD fields — for critical fields (e.g., transdiagnostic CBT fidelity), AI flags rather than infers; human reviewers apply manual scoring.
  • Conservative scoring — absence of public evidence is treated as informative.
  • Source conflict resolution — regulatory database > vendor claim; peer-reviewed publication > press release. Discrepancy logged in Notes field.
  • Reviewer identity — default Research Associate; second reviewers from CDTx team or clinical leads.

6.0 Limitations

6.1 General

  • Language of search — AI searches in English; non-English-marketed apps may be under-represented.
  • Point-in-time — captures Q1-Q2 2026 landscape; re-runs planned before Phase 3.
  • Information asymmetry — vendors may not publicly disclose CBT protocol details, evidence, privacy architecture.
  • Publication bias — clinical evidence relies on published literature.
  • Protocol-specific benchmarks deferred to Phase 3/4.

6.2 AI-Specific

  • Web-accessible only — proprietary data, unpublished studies, confidential regulatory submissions not captured.
  • Temporal web content — date-stamped in extraction record.
  • AI interpretation — borderline cases mitigated by Section 5.3 review.
  • Model knowledge cutoff — supplemented by real-time web search; flagged where AI profile appears thin.
  • Search coverage — very new entrants, pre-commercial prototypes, non-English-marketed apps may be missed; supplemented by Layer 7 expert consultation.

7.0 Outputs and Deliverables

  • Market Scan Workbook (Excel) — coded app profiles by mechanism and symptom; extraction fields per schema; dimension scores with rationale; readiness classification; TDI Strategic Fit; source URLs and access dates. Now includes Evidence sheet with usage/adoption, where CBT shows up, CBT tools/concepts, content/activities, assessments, degree of CBT, elevator pitch, App Store/Google Play URLs.
  • Market Scan Summary Report — narrative synthesis of findings, distribution across readiness tiers, key design patterns per dimension, gap analysis, Phase-3/4 shortlist.
  • Interactive Dashboard — visualisation layer rendering the coded dataset along multiple slice axes (this site).
  • Source Archive — dated archive of source materials per app for audit and reproducibility.
